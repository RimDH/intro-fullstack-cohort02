UI/UX Core Principles

The Foundation of Product Design

UI is the Touchpoint (what you see).
UX is the Journey (how you feel).

Let's dive into the 4 main principles that every developer must master.

Visuals Logic Empathy

1. Visual Hierarchy

Directing the Eye

Users don't read; they scan. Visual hierarchy uses size, color, and whitespace to tell the user what to look at first.

  • Scale: Bigger elements = Higher priority.
  • Color: High contrast = Call to Action.
  • Proximity: Related items should be grouped.

2. Accessibility

Inclusive Development

Web design is for everyone, including users with visual impairments, motor difficulties, or situational limitations.

  • Contrast: Maintain a 4.5:1 ratio for text.
  • Semantic HTML: Use <button>, not <div onclick>.
  • Multi-Modal: Don't use color alone to signal errors.

3. Mental Models

Jakob's Law

Users spend most of their time on other sites. This means they expect your site to work like the ones they already know.

  • Familiarity: Keep search bars and logos in standard spots.
  • Consistency: Buttons should look like buttons globally.
  • Patterns: Use standard icons (Cart, Heart, User).

4. Feedback

Interaction States

Every action needs a reaction. If a user clicks, the system must acknowledge it immediately to reduce uncertainty.

  • Hover/Active: Change colors on mouse-over.
  • Loading: Use skeletons or spinners for data fetching.
  • Success: Clear confirmation of completed tasks.

Exercise 05: Reading

Which alignment is better for reading speed?

Answer: Design B. Left alignment provides a hard "edge" for the eye. In Design A, the eye has to hunt for the start of every new line, which increases fatigue.
DESIGN A

Center alignment makes for a very jagged edge on the left, which makes reading much harder.

DESIGN B

Left alignment is the standard for a reason. It is easier to scan and read long sentences.
